APP encodes the amyloid precursor protein, the protein that is cleaved to produce amyloid-beta fragments. Mutations in APP are a direct cause of early-onset familial Alzheimer's disease.

## Function

APP is a transmembrane protein expressed in neurons and many other cell types. Its normal function is not fully understood, but it appears to play roles in synaptic plasticity, neuronal signaling, and cell adhesion. Crucially, APP is the substrate from which amyloid-beta peptides are generated through sequential cleavage by beta-secretase (BACE1) and gamma-secretase.

## Disease Mechanism

Pathogenic APP mutations typically cluster around the cleavage sites and alter how the protein is processed. Most disease-causing mutations increase the production of longer, more aggregation-prone amyloid-beta species — particularly Aβ42 — or increase the total amount of amyloid-beta produced. These sticky peptides can then aggregate into oligomers and plaques, which are the hallmark extracellular lesion of Alzheimer's disease.

Additionally, gene duplications of APP (which increase total APP expression) cause familial Alzheimer's disease, demonstrating that elevated APP dosage alone is sufficient to drive pathology. Notably, individuals with Down syndrome (trisomy 21) have three copies of chromosome 21, which carries the APP gene, and almost universally develop Alzheimer's pathology by their 40s.

## Clinical Features

APP mutation carriers typically develop symptoms in their 40s to 60s, though the range depends on the specific variant. Disease course resembles typical Alzheimer's with prominent memory impairment, though some mutations are associated with additional features such as cerebral amyloid angiopathy.
