PINK1 is one of the classic genes linked to early-onset Parkinson's disease. PINK1-related disease often resembles typical Parkinson's, with bradykinesia, rigidity, and tremor, but it usually begins much earlier than the more common sporadic form. It is part of the same broader biological pathway as Parkin, which is why the two genes are often discussed together.

## Function

PINK1 encodes a mitochondrial kinase that helps cells monitor mitochondrial health. When mitochondria are damaged, PINK1 accumulates on the outer mitochondrial membrane and helps recruit and activate Parkin, initiating a quality-control program that tags damaged mitochondria for removal through the mitophagy pathway.

## Disease Mechanism

Pathogenic PINK1 mutations usually cause loss of function. This weakens the cell's ability to detect and clear damaged mitochondria, allowing dysfunctional organelles to accumulate, resulting in increased cellular stress, especially in neurons with high energy demand. Because mitochondria are central to ATP production and redox balance, PINK1 deficiency can shift cells into a state of chronic mitochondrial stress that becomes especially harmful in dopaminergic neurons.

## Clinical Features

PINK1-related Parkinson's typically presents earlier than sporadic disease, often in the 30s to 50s. It is autosomal recessive — two mutated copies of the gene are needed to cause disease. The clinical picture closely resembles typical Parkinson's disease, with relatively good levodopa response.
