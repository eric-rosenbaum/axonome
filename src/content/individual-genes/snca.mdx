SNCA is one of the foundational Parkinson's genes because it encodes alpha-synuclein, the protein that aggregates to form Lewy bodies. Rare pathogenic SNCA variants and gene multiplications can cause autosomal dominant Parkinson's disease, and SNCA biology is also deeply relevant to more common sporadic PD.

## Function

Alpha-synuclein is a small protein found especially at the synapses of neurons. It is thought to help regulate the formation of synaptic vesicles and neurotransmitter release. In healthy cells, alpha-synuclein is soluble and functionally integrated into synaptic signaling.

## Disease Mechanism

Disease-causing SNCA changes include missense mutations and gene duplications or triplications. These can increase alpha-synuclein levels or alter its behavior in ways that make it more prone to misfolding and aggregation. SNCA is unusual in showing that both protein structure and protein dosage can drive disease. When there is too much alpha-synuclein or it is mutated, it aggregates and also clogs the cell's waste disposal system.

## Clinical Features

SNCA-related Parkinson's can resemble typical PD, but some forms, especially gene multiplications, are associated with earlier onset and more severe disease, sometimes including prominent cognitive or psychiatric features.

## Broader Relevance

Even without SNCA mutations, alpha-synuclein aggregation is a hallmark of nearly all Parkinson's disease cases and related synucleinopathies. This makes SNCA biology one of the most intensively studied areas in neurodegeneration.
